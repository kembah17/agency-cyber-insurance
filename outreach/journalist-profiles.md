## 5. Response Templates for Common Queries

These templates are starting points — always customize based on the specific query, publication, and journalist's angle.

### Query Type A: "What cyber insurance advice for small businesses?"

**Template Response:**

```
Hi [JOURNALIST NAME],

Happy to help with this one — I've spent the past year researching cyber insurance specifically for small businesses (digital agencies in particular) and published 21 guides on the topic.

Here's my take:

The single most important piece of advice: get a quote before you decide you can't afford it. Most small business owners dramatically overestimate the cost. For a typical 5–50 person company, cyber insurance runs $1,500–$4,000 per year — less than most businesses spend on a single SaaS subscription.

Three specific recommendations:

1. **Start with MFA everywhere.** Multi-factor authentication isn't just good security — it's now a prerequisite for getting quoted by most carriers. Without it, you'll face automatic denial, not just higher premiums. It's free to implement and saves 10–15% on premiums.

2. **Bundle your policies.** Combining cyber liability with your existing Errors & Omissions (E&O) policy saves 15–25% compared to buying them separately. Most small businesses already have E&O — adding cyber to it is the most cost-effective path.

3. **Understand your biggest actual risk.** Most small businesses worry about ransomware, but funds transfer fraud (business email compromise) accounts for 29–39% of all cyber insurance claims — more than any other category. Make sure your policy includes social engineering coverage, and check the sublimit (it's often only $100K–$250K on a $1M policy).

The market is also in buyers' favor right now — premiums declined 2.1% in Q1 2025 after years of increases, and carriers are competing aggressively for SMB business.
```

### Query Type B: "What are the biggest cybersecurity threats in 2025?"

**Template Response:**

```
Hi [JOURNALIST NAME],

Great topic — I can offer a perspective most cybersecurity sources won't: what the insurance claims data actually shows versus what dominates headlines.

The biggest threats based on actual insurance claims (not just media coverage):

1. **Business Email Compromise / Funds Transfer Fraud** — This is the #1 cyber insurance claim category at 29–39% of all claims. An attacker compromises or spoofs an email account and tricks someone into wiring money to a fraudulent account. It's not technically sophisticated, which is exactly why it works. Average loss: $125,000+ per incident.

2. **Ransomware (evolving, not declining)** — Still significant, but the threat has evolved. Attackers now exfiltrate data before encrypting, creating dual extortion scenarios. The insurance angle: carriers increasingly require immutable backups (the 3-2-1-1 standard) and EDR on all endpoints as prerequisites for coverage.

3. **Third-party / Supply Chain Breaches** — Your vendor gets breached, but your customers' data is exposed. This is particularly dangerous for professional services firms (agencies, consultants, MSPs) who handle client credentials and data. Insurance policies are starting to add supply chain breach extensions, but coverage varies widely.

4. **Social Engineering Beyond Email** — Deepfake voice calls, AI-generated phishing, and multi-channel attacks (email + phone + text) are making traditional "verify by phone" procedures less reliable. Insurers are watching this closely — it's the fastest-growing claim category.

What's notable: the threats that generate the most insurance claims aren't the ones that get the most media attention. Ransomware dominates headlines, but BEC/funds transfer fraud costs businesses more in aggregate.
```

### Query Type C: "How should agencies protect client data?"

**Template Response:**

```
Hi [JOURNALIST NAME],

This is right in my wheelhouse — I run a digital agency and spent the past year researching exactly this question, both from a security and insurance perspective.

Here's what I'd recommend for any agency handling client data:

**The non-negotiables (these also determine your insurance eligibility):**

1. **MFA on everything** — Every client account, every internal tool, every email account. This is the #1 security control that cyber insurers evaluate, and without it, most carriers won't even quote you. It's also free to implement.

2. **Separate client credentials from personal ones** — Use a password manager with shared vaults for client access. Never store client passwords in spreadsheets, emails, or browser autofill. When a team member leaves, you need to rotate every credential they had access to.

3. **Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR)** — Traditional antivirus isn't enough anymore. EDR monitors for suspicious behavior patterns, not just known malware signatures. Carriers now require it, and it reduces insurance premiums 10–15%.

**The often-overlooked protections:**

4. **Email authentication (SPF/DKIM/DMARC)** — This prevents attackers from spoofing your agency's email domain to send fraudulent messages to your clients. It's free to set up and most carriers check for it during underwriting.

5. **Dual authorization on financial transactions** — Any payment over $10K should require two people to approve. This single control prevents 90%+ of funds transfer fraud, which is the #1 cyber insurance claim category (29–39% of all claims).

6. **Cyber liability insurance** — Even with perfect security, breaches happen. A policy costs $1,500–$4,000/year for most small agencies and covers breach response, legal costs, client notification, and business interruption. Think of it as the safety net under your security controls.

The key insight from my research: the security controls that protect client data and the controls that reduce insurance premiums are the same list. Investing in security literally pays for itself through lower insurance costs.
```

## 6. Response Best Practices

### Speed Rules

| Platform | Target Response Time | Why |
|----------|---------------------|-----|
| Featured.com | Within 2 hours | AI-scored; early responses get priority |
| Qwoted | Within 3 hours | Journalists set deadlines; early = selected |
| Source of Sources | Within 1 hour | Email format; first responders win |
| MentionMatch | Within 2 hours | Smaller pool; speed is competitive advantage |

### Response Structure (Universal)

1. **Lead with the answer** — Don't bury the insight. First sentence should directly address the journalist's question.
2. **Provide 2–3 specific data points** — Numbers make quotes publishable. Use our verified data (see reference table below).
3. **Add context the journalist might not know** — The "insider" angle is our differentiator.
4. **Keep it under 300 words** — Journalists extract quotes; give them quotable sentences, not essays.
5. **Include your credentials last** — Bio at the bottom, not the top.
6. **Never pitch your site in the response** — Include your URL in your bio/signature only. Let the journalist decide whether to link.

### What Makes Our Responses Stand Out

- **Buyer perspective** — Most cyber insurance sources are brokers or carriers. We're a buyer sharing research.
- **Specific data** — We have real numbers: $1,500–$4,000/year, 29–39% funds transfer fraud, 10–15% MFA savings.
- **Practical advice** — We give actionable steps, not vague recommendations.
- **No sales agenda** — We're not selling insurance. Journalists notice and appreciate this.

### Queries to ALWAYS Respond To

- Anything mentioning "cyber insurance" or "cyber liability"
- Small business cybersecurity advice
- Digital agency / marketing agency risk management
- Data breach costs or response
- Insurance costs for small businesses
- Ransomware coverage or response
- Business email compromise / funds transfer fraud
- Professional liability for service businesses

### Queries to SKIP

- Personal lines insurance (home, auto, health)
- Enterprise-only cybersecurity (Fortune 500 focus)
- Highly technical security topics (zero-day exploits, malware analysis)
- Queries requiring credentials we don't have (CISSP, licensed broker, etc.)

## 7. Platform-Specific Tips

### Featured.com / HARO

- **AI scoring:** Responses are scored by AI for relevance and quality. Be direct, specific, and on-topic.
- **Free tier:** 3 responses per month. Save them for high-DA publications and perfect-fit queries.
- **Paid tier ($19/month):** Unlimited responses. Worth it once you're consistently finding relevant queries.
- **Timing:** Queries go out in batches. Set up email alerts and respond within 2 hours.
- **Format:** Follow their response format exactly. Include your bio, headshot URL, and website.

### Qwoted

- **Vetting:** Qwoted vets experts more strictly. Your profile needs to demonstrate real expertise.
- **Publication quality:** Queries come from higher-tier publications (Forbes, Business Insider, etc.).
- **Relationship building:** Journalists on Qwoted can "favorite" sources. Consistent quality builds repeat relationships.
- **Paid tier ($149/month):** Access to premium queries and priority placement. Consider after 3 months of free tier success.
- **Pro tip:** Qwoted shows journalist profiles. Research the journalist before responding to tailor your angle.

### Source of Sources

- **Email format:** Queries arrive via email newsletter. Speed is critical — first quality response often wins.
- **Founded by Peter Shankman** (original HARO creator). The platform values the same principles: be helpful, be fast, be specific.
- **No profile page:** Your "profile" is your email signature and the quality of your responses.
- **Tip:** Create a dedicated email filter for SoS queries so they don't get buried in your inbox.

### MentionMatch

- **B2B focus:** Queries skew toward marketing, tech, and business topics — perfect for our niche.
- **Best free conversion ratio:** Smaller platform means less competition per query.
- **Format:** Responses are typically shorter and more quote-focused than HARO.
- **Tip:** MentionMatch queries often come from B2B content marketers writing for company blogs. These links may have lower DA but are highly relevant and easier to earn.
